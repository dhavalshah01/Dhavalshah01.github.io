What Prompt Engineering Really Is (and Why Vague Prompts Fail)

March 12, 2026 9 min read Dhaval Shah

If you've ever asked an AI assistant a question and gotten back a confident but utterly useless answer, the problem usually isn't the model — it's the prompt. Prompt engineering is the skill of asking in a way that gets you what you actually want. It's quickly becoming a core literacy, and the good news is the fundamentals are simple enough for anyone to learn. This is the first in a short series; here we cover what prompt engineering is and the single biggest lever you control: specificity.

So what is prompt engineering?

A prompt is just the instruction you give an AI model. Prompt engineering is the practice of crafting and refining that instruction to reliably get a useful result. It isn't coding, and it isn't magic words. At its heart it's clear communication — with one twist: the thing you're communicating with can't see your screen, doesn't know your goal, and won't ask follow-up questions unless you invite it to.

A few ideas do most of the work:

  • Clarity — say exactly what you want, with no room for guessing.
  • Context — give the background the model needs to be relevant to your situation.
  • Constraints — set the guardrails: length, format, tone, audience.
  • Iteration — treat the first answer as a draft and refine, rather than expecting perfection on attempt one.
These principles aren't tied to any one product. Whether you're using a chat assistant, a coding helper, or an image generator, the same habits make your results better. The model changes; good prompting doesn't.

Why "the model can't read your mind"

An AI model predicts a likely response based on the words you give it and the patterns it learned during training. If your words are broad, the most likely response is also broad — generic, safe, and not particularly helpful. You didn't get a bad answer; you got the average answer to a vague question. Specificity is what pulls the model away from the average and toward your actual need.

Here's the same underlying question asked three ways. Watch how the quality of the answer tracks the quality of the prompt:

The prompt quality ladder
Vague
You ask"How do I build a website?"
You get"You can build a website by writing code and hosting it on a server." Technically true, totally unhelpful.
  • No context
  • Too broad
  • Not actionable

You getA nudge toward no-code tools and a rough outline. Closer, but still generic and shallow.
  • Some context
  • Narrows the answer
  • Still lacks depth
Specific
You ask"I'm a beginner with no coding skills. Give me step-by-step instructions to publish a one-page portfolio site for free, listing the tool to use and what to do at each step."
You getA named tool, numbered steps, and a clear path you can follow today.
  • Clear goal & audience
  • Asks for a format
  • Actionable answer

Same question, three rungs of specificity. The answer is only ever as good as the rung you stand on.

The four ingredients you added without noticing

The jump from the first prompt to the third wasn't luck. Four ingredients did the heavy lifting, and you can add them deliberately every time:

  • Who you are — "I'm a beginner with no coding skills" tells the model what level to pitch at.
  • What you want — "publish a one-page portfolio site" is a concrete outcome, not a vague topic.
  • The constraints — "for free" rules out whole categories of answer that would waste your time.
  • The format — "step-by-step instructions... what to do at each step" shapes the answer into something usable.
A quick mental template: "I am [who]. I want [what], given [context/constraints]. Respond as [format]." You won't need all four every time, but reaching for them turns a vague request into a specific one in seconds.

It's a conversation, not a vending machine

The biggest mindset shift is to stop treating the first answer as final. A vending machine gives you one shot; a good assistant improves when you react to it. "That's too technical — explain it for a 10-year-old." "Shorter, just the three key points." "Now give me an example." Each correction is itself a prompt, and refining across a few turns almost always beats agonising over the perfect opening line.

This is why "prompt engineering" has the word engineering in it: not because it's difficult, but because it's iterative. You observe the output, adjust the input, and converge on what you need — the same loop an engineer uses to tune anything.

The takeaway

Prompt engineering isn't a secret handshake. It's clarity, context, constraints, and a willingness to iterate. The fastest way to get better results from any AI tool — today, with no new software — is to climb one rung up the specificity ladder before you hit enter.
