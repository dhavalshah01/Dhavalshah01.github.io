Six Habits of Effective Prompts: A Practical Checklist

April 9, 2026 10 min read Dhaval Shah

In the last article we saw that specificity is the biggest lever you control when prompting an AI. This time we turn that idea into a checklist — six habits you can apply to almost any prompt, whether you're drafting an email, planning a trip, or writing code. None of them require special tools. They just take the guesswork out of what you're asking for.

The six habits at a glance

Think of these as the things a thoughtful colleague would naturally do when handing off a task. Each one removes a different kind of ambiguity:

The effective-prompt checklist
1
Be clear and specific

Spell out the goal, audience, tone, and length. If you want a short answer, say so. If you want expert depth, ask for it.

2
Provide references

Paste the document, data, or example you want the answer based on. Grounded answers are far less likely to be confidently wrong.

3
Break big tasks down

Split a large request into smaller steps. Just like in real work, several small asks beat one giant one — and error rates drop.

4
Let the model think

Ask it to reason step by step before answering. Giving it room to "show its work" leads to noticeably better conclusions.

5
Use the right tool

AI isn't best at everything. For exact math, live data, or precise lookups, use the proper tool and feed the result back in.

6
Check the results

Treat every answer as a draft to verify, especially for facts, figures, and anything you'll act on. Trust, but confirm.

Six habits that remove a different flavour of ambiguity from every prompt you write.

Habit 1: be clear and specific

The model can't read your mind, so describe the output you actually want. Three small additions do most of the work: give details ("a 200-word summary for a non-technical reader"), assign a role ("answer as an experienced travel agent"), and show the shape ("reply as a bulleted list"). If a first answer comes back too long, too shallow, or in the wrong format, say exactly that — naming the problem is itself a clear instruction.

Habit 2: provide references

When you want an answer about a specific document, contract, policy, or dataset, give it to the model rather than hoping it remembers. "Using the text below, summarise the cancellation policy" almost always beats "what's a typical cancellation policy?" References are the single best defence against a confident, made-up answer — and if you ask the model to quote or cite the source, you can check the answer at a glance.

This habit is the seed of a bigger technique called retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), where relevant documents are fetched and added to the prompt automatically. We'll cover it later in the series — but you can do the manual version today simply by pasting in your source material.

Habit 3: break big tasks down

A sprawling request like "plan my entire product launch" invites a shallow, scattered answer. Break it into stages — positioning, then channels, then a week-by-week timeline — and tackle them in turn. Smaller asks are easier for the model to get right, and easier for you to steer. It's the same reason we decompose problems in any kind of project: the whole is disproportionately harder than the sum of its parts.

Habit 4: let the model think

If you ask for a final answer immediately, you often get a rushed one. Add a phrase like "think it through step by step before giving your recommendation," and the model lays out its reasoning first — which tends to make the final answer more accurate. For anything involving logic, comparison, or trade-offs, this one line is the cheapest quality upgrade available.

Habit 5: use the right tool

Language models are remarkable, but they're not calculators, search engines, or databases. For exact arithmetic, the latest prices, or a precise record lookup, use the dedicated tool and paste the result into your prompt as context. Let each tool do what it's best at, and use the AI to interpret, summarise, or explain the result.

Habit 6: check the results

The earlier and smaller your check, the easier problems are to catch. Verify facts, re-run the numbers, and skim for claims that sound too neat. This isn't distrust — it's the same instinct that makes you proofread an important email. A two-minute review turns a plausible draft into something you can confidently stand behind.

Putting it together: before and after

Here's an everyday request rewritten with the habits applied. Notice how the "after" version isn't longer for the sake of it — every addition removes a specific guess the model would otherwise have to make:

The same request, rewritten
Before
"Write a message to my team about the deadline change."
After
"Write a short, friendly email to my 5-person design team letting them know the launch date moved from May 1 to May 15. Reassure them it's to improve quality, and end by inviting questions. Keep it under 120 words."

The highlighted phrases are the habits in action: audience, specifics, tone, intent, and a length constraint.

You won't apply all six habits to every prompt — a quick question doesn't need a reference document. The skill is knowing which ones a given task is missing. When an answer disappoints, run down the list and you'll usually spot the gap.

The takeaway

Great prompting isn't about memorising clever phrases — it's a handful of habits that remove ambiguity before the model ever responds: be specific, provide references, break work down, let it think, use the right tool, and check the output. Internalise these and the quality of everything you get from AI rises.
